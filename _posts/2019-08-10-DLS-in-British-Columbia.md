<b>When describing the early history of mapping North America, it’s necessary to at the very least acknowledge the indigenous people who occupied the continent before European settlement and subsequent efforts to survey and inventory land, with goals to extract natural resources or promote European settlement. Much of the early mapping of the Americas was initiated and conducted without adequate regard for indigenous occupants of the land, including the maps described here. I do not mean to imply that western definitions and descriptions of "North America", "The Dominion", "British Columbia", etc., through surveying and mapping reflect a complete history or rightful "ownership".</b>

Rectangular surveying of North America began in 1785 with the United States' Public Land Survey System (PLSS). and later in Canada in the 1840s under the Dominion Land Survey (DLS). The idea was to divide the land into sections and describe them for new settlement by European Americans.

The typical landscape of British Columbia doesn't cooperate with methods of producing a rectangular survey. Unlike in the Canadian Prairie Provinces and the American Midwest and Great Plains regions, where the horizon stays flat, BC's mountains were a barrier to on-the-ground efforts by new Canadians to inventory land resources.

Rectangular surveying, where straight lines are used to diagram a natural landscape, is a difficult process in BC's generally rugged terrain.
